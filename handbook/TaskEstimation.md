# Task Estimation

## Intro - What is task estimation in scrum?

Task estimation in Scrum is used to understand how much work a task involves so teams can plan their work. However, estimates are often inaccurate due to uncertainty and changing requirements, so they should be treated as a guide rather than a fixed commitment.

This section outlines how our team approaches estimation, including key guidelines for effective estimation, common good and bad practices, and supporting resources for further reading.

---

## Task Estimation Guidelines

### What Task Estimation Should Achieve
- Give the team a shared understanding of the work ahead
- Help plan how much can be delivered in a sprint
- Surface hidden complexity and risks early
- Improve predictability over time through learning

### How We Estimate
- Estimation happens during **sprint planning**
- The **whole team** participates — not just the assigned developer
- We use **story points** to measure relative complexity, not hours
- Tasks are compared to previously completed work to calibrate estimates

> **Rule**
> - Estimate complexity and effort, not time
> - A story point means the same thing across the whole team

### Estimation Techniques

**Planning Poker**
- Each team member votes on a story point value independently
- Cards are revealed simultaneously to avoid bias
- Differences in votes are discussed until consensus is reached
- Prevents one strong voice from dominating

**Story Points**
- Measure complexity, risk and effort combined
- Use a fixed scale (e.g. 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13)
- Relative to each other — a 5 should feel roughly twice as complex as a 3
- Do not convert story points to hours

**T-Shirt Sizing**
- Use for high level estimation early in planning (XS, S, M, L, XL)
- Useful when tasks are too vague for story points yet
- Helps the team quickly prioritise the backlog

### Planning Poker Flow
```
Introduce Story → Individual Voting → Reveal Cards → Discuss Differences → Reach Consensus → Assign Points
```

### Writing Good Estimates
- **Break tasks down** — if a task feels too large to estimate confidently, split it
- **Estimate as a team** — all perspectives (dev, QA, design) should be heard
- **Use past sprints** — reference completed work to ground your estimates in reality
- **Timebox debates** — if the team cannot agree after discussion, default to the higher estimate

### Reviewing Estimates
- After each sprint, compare estimates to what actually happened
- Look for patterns — are certain task types always underestimated?
- Use retrospectives to refine the process, not to assign blame
- Accuracy improves over time — new teams should expect early estimates to be off

### Key Principle
> Estimation is not about being right — it is about building a shared understanding 
> of the work and improving sprint by sprint.


## Good Practices

**Break large tasks into smaller pieces**
- If a task is too large to estimate confidently, it should be split into smaller tasks, which allows tasks to be estimated and tracked during a sprint.

**Encourage discussion during estimation**
- Differences in estimates should lead to discussion. This helps uncover misunderstandings or hidden work before the sprint begins.

**Using past sprint data to help guide estimates**
- HHaving previous work can help the team understand what a story point actually represents and improve consistency over time.


## Bad Practices

**Estimating tasks that are poorly defined**
- If some requirements are unclear or throughout, estimates will likely be inaccurate.

**Estimating tasks indivdually without team input**
- If only one developer estimates work, important complexity or dependencies may be missed.

**Ignoring estimation mistakes**
- Teams should review past sprints and learn from estimation inaccuracies instead of repeating the same mistakes again.
